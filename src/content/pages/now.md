# Now

_Last updated: May 13, 2026_

I’m currently focused on building across four overlapping areas: manufacturing, software, international expansion, and practical infrastructure.

## What I’m working on

### Chunhui’s international presence

I’m working on how Chunhui should present itself to global customers, especially in HVAC, hydronic heating, and industrial manufacturing markets.

A lot of my current thinking is around:

- how to explain technical products clearly to overseas buyers
- how to structure product pages, landing pages, and SEO content
- how to choose better English terminology for Chinese manufacturing categories
- how to build trust through a more professional international website
- how to connect product capability with customer-facing proof

The goal is not just “make a website,” but to make Chunhui easier to understand, easier to trust, and easier to buy from.

### Manufacturing and semiconductor-related research

I’ve been digging deeper into semiconductor and advanced manufacturing components, especially ceramic heaters, wafer heaters, electrostatic chucks, thermocouples, RTDs, and flexible heaters.

Recently I’ve been looking more closely at how Chinese semiconductor component suppliers are positioned: who has real production capacity, who is moving from technical validation into scale, and how products like ESCs and ceramic heaters fit together inside equipment architecture.

This connects to a broader interest of mine: understanding how advanced manufacturing companies communicate technical capability, product reliability, and engineering depth.

I like digging into these niche industrial categories because the surface looks boring, but the real business value is usually hidden in the details.

### Internal tools, CRM, and project systems

On the software side, I’m building and refining tools for project management, issue tracking, analytics, and internal business operations.

Recently I’ve been reviewing CRM and invoice workflows, including pending business invoices for semiconductor customers like Lam Research and Tokyo Electron. I’m also improving internal training and knowledge modules around semi-related business processes.

I’m still interested in dashboards inspired by tools like Linear and Swarmia: not just listing tasks, but helping teams see delivery speed, ownership, bottlenecks, follow-ups, and progress more clearly.

I care about software that actually changes how people work, not dashboards that only look nice.

### Network infrastructure and self-hosted workflows

I’ve also been spending time on practical infrastructure: router configuration, DNS behavior, proxy profiles, OpenClash, Surge, SmartDNS, and iStoreOS.

Recently this meant migrating managed proxy configs into custom profiles, debugging subscription/profile merging issues, translating Surge host rules into SmartDNS rules, and making sure local DNS and proxy behavior are safer and more predictable.

This kind of work is messy, but I like it because it sits right where software meets real-world operations: configuration, reliability, security, and “why is this thing silently breaking?”

### AI-native workflows

I’m experimenting with AI agents, sandbox environments, automation workflows, and ways to turn scattered work into structured output.

The direction I’m interested in is: how can AI become part of real work systems, not just a chat box?

That means exploring things like:

- coding agents
- isolated execution environments
- automatic documentation
- internal productivity tools
- better ways to summarize, remember, and reuse daily work

## What I’m thinking about

I’m thinking a lot about how traditional manufacturing companies can become more global, more digital, and more legible.

Many good companies already have the product capability, but they don’t explain themselves well enough.

That gap is interesting to me.

If a company has strong engineering, stable production, and real experience, then the next challenge is making that strength visible to the right people.

I’m also thinking about how much of useful work happens in the unglamorous middle layer: cleaning up data, fixing workflows, debugging infrastructure, writing clearer explanations, and connecting one system to another.

That middle layer is where a lot of leverage hides.
